What this score means

How to read the number

  • Higher = better biometric quality — sharpness, pose, lighting, occlusion — not beauty or “good photo” vibes.
  • Most faces score ~20–26. That is normal, not failure. Scores above ~28 are uncommon even on FFHQ. Above 30 is rare (~1% of labels).
  • Scale is ~11–33 native units, not 0–100. A 22 is mid-pack; an 18 is below average; a 15 is poor.
  • Approximate, not exact OFIQ. Average error is about ±1.32 on held-out FFHQ (Pearson r ≈ 0.87). Extremes regress toward the mean.
  • Crop matters. The network was trained on face crops. We show the crop we scored below — if it’s wrong, the score will be wrong.

About this project

The problem

OFIQ (Open Source Face Image Quality) measures how suitable a face image is for biometric matching — combining sharpness, head pose, illumination, exposure, and occlusion into a single UnifiedQualityScore. It is accurate but slow (seconds per image).

This project trains a lightweight SmallResNet CNN (~0.33M parameters) to predict that score from pixels alone, achieving millisecond inference while keeping strong correlation with OFIQ on 7,000 held-out test faces (MAE 1.32, Spearman ρ 0.87).

Why it matters

Fast, differentiable quality estimation enables large-scale filtering of face datasets and could serve as a critic inside generative face pipelines — pushing synthetic images toward higher biometric usability without running full OFIQ on every sample.

The model was trained on 70,000 FFHQ portraits with OFIQ labels, using heavy regularization (spatial dropout, SE attention, combined MSE+Pearson loss) to avoid the catastrophic overfitting seen with off-the-shelf ResNet18.
